# Knowledge Distillation for Large Language Models

How to transfer the capabilities of a large "teacher" model into a smaller, cheaper "student" model — from Hinton's 2015 temperature trick to modern LLM distillation pipelines.

## Background

**Foundational paper**: [Distilling the Knowledge in a Neural Network](https://arxiv.org/abs/1503.02531) (Hinton, Vinyals & Dean, Google, 2015)

**Research lineage** — Knowledge distillation evolved through several eras:

1. **Hinton et al. (2015)** — Introduced the core framework: train a small "student" network to match the softened output distribution of a large "teacher" network. The key insight: the teacher's soft probability distribution over classes (what Hinton calls "dark knowledge") carries far more information than hard labels alone. A teacher that assigns P("cat")=0.7, P("dog")=0.2 is telling the student that cats and dogs are visually similar — signal that hard one-hot labels destroy.

2. **DistilBERT** (Sanh et al., Hugging Face, 2019, [arxiv 1910.01108](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.01108)) — Applied distillation to pretrained Transformers for the first time at scale. Distilled BERT-base into a model 40% smaller that retained 97% of BERT's language understanding while being 60% faster. Used a triple loss: masked language modeling + KL divergence on soft targets + cosine distance between hidden states.

3. **TinyBERT** (Jiao et al., 2019, [arxiv 1909.10351](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.10351)) — Went beyond output distillation to **feature distillation**: matching intermediate Transformer layers (attention matrices and hidden states) between teacher and student. A 4-layer TinyBERT achieved 96.8% of BERT-base performance while being 7.5x smaller and 9.4x faster. Introduced two-stage distillation: general pretraining distillation + task-specific distillation.

4. **Orca** (Mukherjee et al., Microsoft, 2023, [arxiv 2306.02707](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.02707)) — Shifted to **response distillation** for LLMs. Instead of matching logits or hidden states, Orca trained a 13B model on GPT-4's detailed chain-of-thought outputs ("explanation tuning"). The key: previous distillation attempts produced models that imitated the teacher's *style* but not its *reasoning*. Orca fixed this by collecting responses where GPT-4 explained its step-by-step reasoning process. Orca-13B surpassed Vicuna-13B by >100% on Big-Bench Hard.

5. **Phi series** (Gunasekar, Li et al., Microsoft, 2023-2024, [arxiv 2306.11644](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.11644)) — Pioneered "distillation through data curation." Phi-1 (1.3B) was pretrained on synthetic "textbook-quality" data generated by GPT-3.5/GPT-4, achieving coding performance competitive with models 10x its size. Phi-2 scaled this to general knowledge with 20B tokens of synthetic textbooks. Phi-4 ([arxiv 2412.08905](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.08905)) went further — a 14B model that **surpassed its teacher GPT-4o** on STEM benchmarks, demonstrating that synthetic data distillation can go beyond mere imitation.

6. **DeepSeek-R1 distillation** (DeepSeek-AI, January 2025, [arxiv 2501.12948](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948)) — Distilled a 671B MoE reasoning model into 1.5B-70B dense students via straightforward SFT on the teacher's chain-of-thought outputs. The distilled 7B model outperformed QwQ-32B-Preview on AIME; the distilled 32B surpassed OpenAI o1-mini. Proved that distillation from a strong reasoning teacher is more effective than training RL from scratch on smaller models.

**The evolution**: Classic KD (match logits) → Feature KD (match internals) → Response KD (train on teacher outputs) → Reasoning KD (train on teacher's chain-of-thought) → Synthetic data KD (train on teacher-generated curricula). Each step required less access to the teacher's internals while achieving comparable or better results.

## What Problem Does Distillation Solve?

Large models are expensive. GPT-4-class models cost millions to train and dollars per million tokens to serve. A 70B model needs multiple GPUs for inference. A 671B MoE model is impractical for most deployment scenarios.

But most of a large model's knowledge can be transferred to a much smaller model:

```
Model                          AIME 2024    Inference Cost    Hardware
───────────────────────────    ─────────    ──────────────    ──────────────
DeepSeek-R1 (671B MoE)        79.8%        $$$               Multi-GPU cluster
R1-Distill-Qwen-32B           72.6%        $$                Single A100/H100
R1-Distill-Qwen-7B            55.5%        $                 Single consumer GPU
R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B          28.9%        ¢                 Phone / edge device
```

The 32B distilled model captures ~91% of the full R1's AIME performance at a fraction of the cost. The 7B model — runnable on a single consumer GPU — outperforms QwQ-32B-Preview (a 32B model that wasn't distilled from R1).

Distillation is the primary mechanism by which frontier capabilities become accessible to the broader community. When DeepSeek released their distilled models, anyone with a laptop could run a 7B model that outperforms much larger models on math reasoning.

## Key Terms

**Teacher model**: The large, capable model whose knowledge you want to transfer. Can be a proprietary API (GPT-4), an open-weight model (Llama 405B), or a specialized model (DeepSeek-R1).

**Student model**: The smaller model being trained. Typically 1/10th to 1/100th the size of the teacher.

**Hard labels**: Standard one-hot targets. For classification: [0, 0, 1, 0]. For language modeling: the actual next token. Contains no information about the teacher's uncertainty.

**Soft labels / soft targets**: The teacher's full probability distribution over all tokens/classes. Contains "dark knowledge" — the relative probabilities of non-top tokens carry information about similarity structure.

**Temperature (T)**: A scalar that softens the teacher's output distribution. Higher T → more uniform distribution → more information about inter-class similarities. T=1 is the standard softmax; T=20 makes the distribution very smooth.

**Logit distillation**: Matching the student's output distribution to the teacher's using KL divergence on softened logits. Requires access to teacher logits (white-box).

**Feature distillation**: Matching intermediate representations (hidden states, attention maps) between teacher and student. Requires access to teacher internals.

**Response distillation**: Training the student via SFT on the teacher's generated text outputs. Only requires API access to the teacher (black-box). The simplest and most common approach for LLMs.

**KL divergence**: Measures how one probability distribution diverges from another. KL(P || Q) = Σ P(x) log(P(x)/Q(x)). Not symmetric: KL(teacher || student) ≠ KL(student || teacher). The direction matters — more on this below.

**Alpha (α)**: Interpolation weight between distillation loss and standard cross-entropy loss. Balances learning from the teacher vs learning from ground-truth labels.

## Classic Knowledge Distillation (Hinton et al., 2015)

### The Temperature Trick

Standard softmax converts logits to probabilities:

```
softmax(z_i) = e^(z_i) / Σ_j e^(z_j)
```

Softmax with **temperature T** divides logits by T before exponentiating:

```
softmax(z_i / T) = e^(z_i/T) / Σ_j e^(z_j/T)
```

Higher T → softer distribution. Lower T → sharper distribution. T=1 is standard softmax.

### Numerical Walkthrough: Why Temperature Matters

Suppose a teacher classifying an image produces logits:

```
Class:     cat     dog     car     tree
Logits:    5.0     3.0    -1.0    -2.0
```

**At T=1 (standard softmax)**:
```
e^5.0 = 148.4    e^3.0 = 20.1    e^-1.0 = 0.37    e^-2.0 = 0.14
Sum = 169.0

P(cat)  = 148.4 / 169.0 = 0.878
P(dog)  = 20.1  / 169.0 = 0.119
P(car)  = 0.37  / 169.0 = 0.002
P(tree) = 0.14  / 169.0 = 0.001
```

The distribution is very peaked — almost all the probability mass is on "cat". The student learns "this is a cat" but gets very little signal about relative similarities.

**At T=5 (softened)**:
```
z/T:     1.0     0.6    -0.2    -0.4

e^1.0 = 2.72    e^0.6 = 1.82    e^-0.2 = 0.82    e^-0.4 = 0.67
Sum = 6.03

P(cat)  = 2.72 / 6.03 = 0.451
P(dog)  = 1.82 / 6.03 = 0.302
P(car)  = 0.82 / 6.03 = 0.136
P(tree) = 0.67 / 6.03 = 0.111
```

Now the distribution reveals structure: the teacher thinks "dog" (0.302) is much more similar to "cat" than "car" (0.136) or "tree" (0.111). This is "dark knowledge" — the inter-class similarity structure that hard labels destroy.

**At T=20 (very soft)**:
```
z/T:     0.25    0.15   -0.05   -0.10

P(cat)  = 0.271
P(dog)  = 0.245
P(car)  = 0.253
P(tree) = 0.240
```

Too soft — the distribution approaches uniform, losing the original signal. The sweet spot is typically T=2 to T=20 depending on the task and teacher-student size gap.

### The KD Loss Function

```
L_KD = α × T² × KL(softmax(z_teacher/T) || softmax(z_student/T)) + (1-α) × CE(y_hard, z_student)
```

Breaking this down:

1. **KL divergence term**: The student's softened distribution should match the teacher's softened distribution. This is the "learn from the teacher" component.

2. **Cross-entropy term**: The student should also predict the correct hard label. This is the "learn from ground truth" component.

3. **α**: Controls the balance. Typical values: 0.5-0.9 (favoring the teacher signal).

4. **T²**: A scaling factor. When temperature T is used, the gradients from the soft targets are scaled by 1/T², so multiplying by T² ensures the distillation gradient and the hard-label gradient are on comparable scales.

### Why Soft Labels Carry More Information

Consider the LLM case. A teacher model predicting the next token after "The capital of France is" produces:

```
Token:          Paris    Lyon    Berlin    pizza
T=1 probs:      0.92     0.03    0.02     0.0001
Hard label:     1        0       0         0
T=3 probs:      0.55     0.16    0.13     0.01
```

Hard labels say: "Paris is right, everything else is equally wrong." But the teacher's soft distribution reveals:
- Lyon (another French city) has higher probability than Berlin — geographical relevance
- Berlin (a European capital) has higher probability than pizza — semantic category
- These relative rankings encode the teacher's world knowledge

The student learning from soft labels absorbs these relationships. Learning from hard labels alone, it would need to discover them independently from pretraining data.

### Implementation

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def kd_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, hard_labels, T=4.0, alpha=0.7):
    """
    Classic Hinton knowledge distillation loss.

    Args:
        student_logits: (batch, vocab_size) raw student output
        teacher_logits: (batch, vocab_size) raw teacher output
        hard_labels: (batch,) ground-truth token ids
        T: temperature for softening distributions
        alpha: weight for distillation loss vs hard label loss
    """
    # Soft targets from teacher (detached — no gradient through teacher)
    soft_teacher = F.softmax(teacher_logits / T, dim=-1)

    # Soft predictions from student
    log_soft_student = F.log_softmax(student_logits / T, dim=-1)

    # KL divergence: teacher || student
    # T² scaling compensates for gradient magnitude reduction
    distill_loss = F.kl_div(log_soft_student, soft_teacher, reduction='batchmean') * (T ** 2)

    # Standard cross-entropy on hard labels
    hard_loss = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, hard_labels)

    return alpha * distill_loss + (1 - alpha) * hard_loss
```

## Modern LLM Distillation Methods

Classic KD requires access to the teacher's logits — you need to run the teacher model and extract its output distribution. For proprietary models (GPT-4, Claude), this is impossible. For open models, it requires enough GPU memory to run both teacher and student simultaneously.

Modern LLM distillation has evolved several approaches with different access requirements:

### 1. Response Distillation (SFT on Teacher Outputs)

The simplest approach: generate text from the teacher, then SFT the student on it. This is what Orca, Phi, and most practitioners do.

```
Teacher (GPT-4) ──generates──→ Dataset of (prompt, response) pairs ──SFT──→ Student (7B)
```

**Why it works**: When the teacher generates a detailed response — especially one with step-by-step reasoning — the text itself encodes the teacher's knowledge. The student learning to produce similar text absorbs the reasoning patterns, factual knowledge, and response style.

**What you need**: API access to the teacher. No logits, no weights, no internals.

**Limitations**:
- **Distribution mismatch**: The student is trained on the teacher's outputs but generates its own at inference time. If the student makes an error early in generation, it enters a distribution it never saw during training, leading to cascading errors. GKD addresses this (see below).
- **Ceiling effect**: The student can only learn what the teacher explicitly writes. Implicit knowledge — what the teacher "knows" but doesn't express in any particular response — is lost.
- **Style vs substance**: Without care, the student may learn to imitate the teacher's writing style rather than its reasoning. Orca's "explanation tuning" was designed to fix this.

**Practical code for response distillation**:

```python
from trl import SFTTrainer, SFTConfig
from datasets import load_dataset

# Dataset format: {"prompt": "...", "completion": "..."} where completion is teacher-generated
dataset = load_dataset("your_teacher_generated_data")

config = SFTConfig(
    per_device_train_batch_size=4,
    learning_rate=2e-5,
    num_train_epochs=3,
    max_seq_length=2048,
    logging_steps=10,
)

trainer = SFTTrainer(
    model=student_model,
    args=config,
    train_dataset=dataset,
    processing_class=tokenizer,
)

trainer.train()
```

### 2. Logit Distillation (Full Distribution Matching)

When you have access to teacher logits, you can use the full Hinton-style KL divergence loss. This gives the student richer signal than response distillation alone.

**Key papers**:

- **MiniLLM** (Gu et al., 2024, [arxiv 2306.08543](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.08543), ICLR 2024) — Key insight: standard forward KL divergence (KL(teacher || student)) makes the student assign probability mass everywhere the teacher does, leading to overly smooth outputs. MiniLLM uses **reverse KL** (KL(student || teacher)) instead, which encourages the student to focus on the teacher's high-probability regions, producing more precise and focused responses. Results: better calibration, lower exposure bias, and higher quality in long-form generation.

- **DistiLLM** (Ko et al., 2024, [arxiv 2402.03898](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.03898), ICML 2024) — Introduces **skew KL divergence**, which interpolates between forward and reverse KL, avoiding the extreme behavior of either. Also adds an adaptive off-policy approach for efficiently reusing student-generated outputs. Achieves up to 4.3x speedup over prior KD methods.

**Forward vs Reverse KL — why direction matters**:

```
Forward KL: KL(P_teacher || Q_student) = Σ P(x) log(P(x)/Q(x))
  → Student must cover ALL modes of teacher distribution
  → Leads to mode-averaging: student spreads probability across everything
  → Good for: not missing any teacher knowledge

Reverse KL: KL(Q_student || P_teacher) = Σ Q(x) log(Q(x)/P(x))
  → Student can focus on HIGH-PROBABILITY modes
  → Leads to mode-seeking: student picks the teacher's peaks
  → Good for: producing sharp, precise outputs

Skew KL (DistiLLM): interpolates between the two
  → α × forward_KL + (1-α) × reverse_KL
  → Balances coverage and precision
```

For autoregressive LLMs, reverse KL or skew KL tends to work better than forward KL because we want the student to generate high-quality text (mode-seeking) rather than hedge across all possibilities (mode-averaging).

### 3. Chain-of-Thought Distillation

Distilling the teacher's *reasoning process*, not just its final answers. The student learns to think step by step by imitating the teacher's chain-of-thought.

**The DeepSeek-R1 approach**: Generate 800k chain-of-thought reasoning traces from R1, filter to only correct outputs (rejection sampling), then SFT smaller models on this data. The students learned not just to get the right answers, but to produce extended reasoning, self-verification, and error correction — behaviors the teacher developed through RL.

**Why it's different from basic response distillation**: Standard response distillation on short answers (e.g., "The answer is 42") gives the student no reasoning signal. CoT distillation on extended traces (e.g., "Let me break this down... First... Therefore... Let me verify... The answer is 42") teaches the student a reasoning *process*.

**Keypoint-based Progressive CoT Distillation (KPOD)** (Feng et al., 2024, ICML 2024) — Recognized that not all tokens in a reasoning chain are equally important. KPOD uses a learned mask to identify "keypoint tokens" (the critical reasoning steps) and trains the student progressively: first learn to generate the final steps, then gradually extend to cover the full chain. This is more efficient than forcing the student to match every token equally.

**Curriculum-based CoT distillation** — Trains the student on progressively harder problems. Start with simple reasoning chains (2-3 steps), then move to complex ones (10+ steps). A 770M T5 model trained this way reached 94% of a 540B teacher's performance on the SVAMP math benchmark.

### 4. On-Policy Distillation (GKD)

**Paper**: [On-Policy Distillation of Language Models: Learning from Self-Generated Mistakes](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.13649) (Agarwal et al., Google DeepMind, ICLR 2024)

The core problem with off-policy distillation (training on teacher-generated data):

```
Training time:  Student sees teacher's outputs → learns to continue teacher-like text
Inference time: Student generates its own tokens → may diverge from teacher distribution
                If token 5 is wrong, tokens 6+ are out-of-distribution → cascading errors
```

GKD's insight: train the student on its **own** generated outputs, using the teacher to score them. This is the same on-policy vs off-policy distinction from RL — and the same solution.

```
Off-policy:  Teacher generates → Student learns on teacher's text → distribution mismatch at inference
On-policy:   Student generates → Teacher scores → Student learns on its own text → no mismatch
```

**How GKD works**:
1. Student generates a response to a prompt
2. Teacher provides the target distribution (soft labels) for the student's generated sequence
3. Student updates to match the teacher's distribution on its own tokens
4. Repeat

The student learns to correct its own mistakes rather than memorize the teacher's outputs. GKD also supports alternative divergence measures (reverse KL, JSD) when the student lacks capacity to fully mimic the teacher.

**Results**: GKD improves on standard KD for summarization, translation, and arithmetic reasoning. It also integrates cleanly with RLHF — you can distill and align simultaneously.

### 5. Progressive Distillation

Instead of distilling directly from a very large teacher to a very small student, distill through intermediate stages:

```
671B teacher → 70B student₁ → 14B student₂ → 7B student₃
```

Each step has a smaller teacher-student gap, making the distillation easier. The intuition: a 7B model may not have enough capacity to directly absorb a 671B model's knowledge, but it can learn from a 14B model that already distilled the essentials.

**Progressive Overload Curriculum Learning (POCL, 2025)** — Adapts the progressive distillation idea to the data dimension. Instead of progressively shrinking the model, it progressively increases data difficulty: start training the student on easy samples, then gradually add harder ones. The student model itself assesses sample difficulty, creating an adaptive curriculum.

### 6. Speculative Knowledge Distillation (SKD, 2025)

A recent technique that connects distillation to speculative decoding — the inference-time technique where a small "draft" model generates candidate tokens that a large "verifier" model accepts or rejects.

**SKD** ([ICLR 2025](https://proceedings.iclr.cc/paper_files/paper/2025/file/a2747a3844ca1e4667fbff3f558eb39b-Paper-Conference.pdf)) filters out low-quality student tokens during training by replacing them with teacher tokens, then progressively accepts more student tokens as training proceeds. Early in training, the student is mostly learning from teacher tokens (like off-policy distillation). Late in training, the student generates most tokens itself (like on-policy distillation). This smooth transition avoids the cold-start problem of pure on-policy methods.

**SpecKD** — Trains domain-specific draft models for speculative decoding, using knowledge distillation to improve the draft model's token acceptance rate. This reduces inference latency without increasing draft model size.

## DeepSeek-R1 Distillation Case Study

The most impactful LLM distillation result to date. (If you've read the DeepSeek-R1 guide, this section goes deeper on the distillation-specific details.)

### Process

1. **Teacher**: DeepSeek-R1 (671B MoE), specifically the Stage 3 checkpoint (after rejection sampling + SFT)
2. **Data generation**: R1 generated reasoning chains for ~800k diverse prompts spanning math, code, science, logic, and general QA
3. **Filtering**: Only correct, high-quality outputs were kept (rejection sampling — generate multiple, keep the correct ones)
4. **Student training**: Standard SFT on this filtered dataset. Student base models: Qwen2.5 (1.5B, 7B, 14B, 32B) and Llama-3 (8B, 70B)

No RL was applied to the student models. Pure SFT on the teacher's outputs.

### Results

```

─────────────────────────────  ─────────    ────────    ────────────    ─────────────
DeepSeek-R1 (671B teacher)     79.8%        97.3%       71.5%           65.9%
─────────────────────────────  ─────────    ────────    ────────────    ─────────────
R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B          28.9%        83.9%       —               —
R1-Distill-Qwen-7B            55.5%        92.8%       49.1%           37.6%
R1-Distill-Llama-8B           50.4%        89.1%       49.0%           39.6%
R1-Distill-Qwen-14B           69.7%        93.9%       59.1%           53.1%
R1-Distill-Qwen-32B           72.6%        94.3%       62.1%           57.2%
R1-Distill-Llama-70B          70.0%        94.5%       65.2%           57.5%
─────────────────────────────  ─────────    ────────    ────────────    ─────────────
Comparisons:
```

**Standout findings**:
- **7B beats 32B**: R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (55.5% AIME) surpasses QwQ-32B-Preview (50.0%) — a model 4.5x larger that was trained with RL, not distilled. The quality of the teacher matters more than the size of the student.
- **32B beats o1-mini**: R1-Distill-Qwen-32B (72.6% AIME) surpasses OpenAI o1-mini (63.6%) — an open-weight model beating a frontier closed model.
- **1.5B on a phone**: Even the 1.5B model gets 83.9% on MATH-500, substantially outperforming GPT-4o on this benchmark.
- **Diminishing returns above 32B**: The 70B model only marginally improves over 32B (and slightly regresses on AIME), suggesting the Qwen-32B architecture is near the distillation ceiling for this data.

### Distillation vs RL from Scratch

The paper ran a critical experiment: train Qwen2.5-32B with GRPO from scratch (no distillation, >10k RL steps). Result: the RL-trained model underperformed the distilled model on every benchmark.

```
Method                               AIME 2024    MATH-500
────────────────────────────────     ─────────    ────────
R1-Distill-Qwen-32B (distillation)   72.6%        94.3%
R1-Zero-Qwen-32B (RL from scratch)   lower        lower
```

**Why distillation wins at smaller scales**: RL-discovered reasoning requires massive model capacity — R1-Zero needed 671B parameters to independently discover chain-of-thought, self-verification, and backtracking. A 32B model doesn't have enough capacity to discover these strategies from scratch through RL alone. But it has enough capacity to *imitate* them once shown examples. Distillation provides those examples.

This matches a general principle: **discovery requires scale; imitation is cheap**. The teacher paid the compute cost to discover the strategies. The student just needs to learn to reproduce them.

## Phi Series Case Study

Microsoft's Phi models pioneered a different distillation philosophy: instead of training on the teacher's responses to user prompts, generate an entire **curriculum** of synthetic training data.

### The Approach

**Phi-1** (2023, [arxiv 2306.11644](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.11644), "Textbooks Are All You Need"):
- 1.3B parameter model for code generation
- Pretrained on ~7B tokens: filtered web data + ~1B tokens of synthetic "textbooks" and exercises generated by GPT-3.5
- A quality classifier filtered web code to "textbook-quality" — clear, self-contained, educational
- The synthetic data was specifically designed to be pedagogical: building concepts incrementally, covering diverse programming patterns
- Result: Phi-1 at 1.3B matched or exceeded models 10x its size on HumanEval

**Phi-2** (2023):
- 2.7B parameters
- 20B tokens of synthetic textbooks on common-sense reasoning and general knowledge
- Matched Llama-2-70B on some benchmarks despite being 25x smaller

**Phi-3** (2024):
- 3.8B parameters
- Improved data filtering and curation pipeline
- Competitive with much larger models across broad benchmarks

**Phi-4** (2024, [arxiv 2412.08905](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.08905)):
- 14B parameters
- **Surpassed its teacher GPT-4o** on GPQA (graduate-level STEM) and MATH benchmarks
- The research team's own assessment: Phi-4's performance "goes beyond distillation" — the synthetic data pipeline generates training signal that the teacher model itself doesn't fully exploit in individual responses

### Why This Works

The Phi approach is distillation through **data curation**, not through logits or response imitation:

```
Traditional distillation:  Teacher model → teacher's responses → student learns responses
Phi approach:              Teacher model → teacher generates curriculum → student learns from curriculum
```

The difference is subtle but important. When GPT-4 generates a textbook chapter on recursion, it's not just answering a question — it's creating structured educational content that covers the concept systematically, with progressive examples, edge cases, and connections to related concepts. The student model trained on this curriculum absorbs knowledge more efficiently than it would from scattered Q&A pairs.

**Key insight**: The quality and structure of training data matters more than quantity. 7B tokens of well-structured synthetic data beats 1T tokens of web scrape. This is because the synthetic data has:
- No noise, no contradictions, no irrelevant content
- Progressive difficulty (building concepts step by step)
- Comprehensive coverage (the teacher can be prompted to fill knowledge gaps)
- Consistent quality (every example is "textbook-grade")

### The Student Surpasses the Teacher

Phi-4 surpassing GPT-4o on STEM benchmarks is a remarkable result. How can a student outperform its teacher?

The teacher generates thousands of diverse training examples across a domain. No single response captures all of the teacher's knowledge, but the *aggregate* of thousands of responses covers the domain more thoroughly than any individual interaction. The student, trained on this comprehensive dataset, develops a more consistent and complete understanding of the domain than the teacher exhibits in any single query.

This is analogous to how a student who reads every lecture transcript might perform better on an exam than the professor answering a single question — the student has absorbed the professor's knowledge across all lectures.

## When to Use Which Method

| Method | Teacher Access | Best For | Quality | Difficulty | Compute |
|--------|---------------|----------|---------|------------|---------|
| **Response distillation (SFT)** | API only (black-box) | General capabilities, quick results | Good | Easy | Low |
| **Logit distillation (KL div)** | Full logits (white-box) | Maximum knowledge transfer | Best | Medium | High |
| **CoT distillation** | API only (black-box) | Reasoning tasks (math, code, logic) | Very good for reasoning | Easy | Medium |
| **Synthetic data generation** | API only (black-box) | Pre-training, broad knowledge | Very good | Medium | High (data gen) |
| **On-policy (GKD)** | Full logits (white-box) | Avoiding distribution mismatch | Best for deployment | Hard | High |
| **Progressive distillation** | Full model (white-box) | Very large teacher-student gap | Good | Medium | Very high |

**Decision flowchart**:

```
Do you have access to teacher logits?
├─ Yes → Logit distillation (MiniLLM, DistiLLM, GKD)
│        Best quality. Use on-policy (GKD) if deployment robustness matters.
│
└─ No (API only) →
   │
   ├─ Is the task reasoning-heavy (math, code, logic)?
   │  └─ Yes → CoT distillation
   │           Generate teacher reasoning traces, filter for correctness, SFT student
   │           (This is the DeepSeek-R1 distillation approach)
   │
   ├─ Do you need broad pre-training knowledge?
   │  └─ Yes → Synthetic data generation (Phi approach)
   │           Generate textbook-quality curricula, pretrain student on them
   │
   └─ General instruction-following?
      └─ Response distillation (SFT on teacher outputs)
         The simplest approach. Start here, upgrade if needed.
```

## Practical Implementation

### Classic KD Loss with Temperature (White-Box)

Use this when you have access to both teacher and student logits — for example, distilling an open-weight 70B into a 7B.

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

class DistillationTrainer:
    def __init__(self, student, teacher, tokenizer, T=4.0, alpha=0.7, lr=2e-5):
        self.student = student
        self.teacher = teacher.eval()  # Frozen
        self.tokenizer = tokenizer
        self.T = T
        self.alpha = alpha
        self.optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(student.parameters(), lr=lr)

        # Freeze teacher — no gradients
        for p in self.teacher.parameters():
            p.requires_grad = False

    def train_step(self, input_ids, attention_mask, labels):
        # Student forward
        student_out = self.student(input_ids=input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask)
        student_logits = student_out.logits  # (batch, seq_len, vocab_size)

        # Teacher forward (no grad)
        with torch.no_grad():
            teacher_out = self.teacher(input_ids=input_ids, attention_mask=attention_mask)
            teacher_logits = teacher_out.logits

        # Only compute loss on non-padding positions
        mask = labels != -100  # (batch, seq_len)

        # Flatten for loss computation
        s_logits = student_logits[mask]   # (N, vocab_size)
        t_logits = teacher_logits[mask]   # (N, vocab_size)
        flat_labels = labels[mask]        # (N,)

        # KD loss: soft target matching
        soft_student = F.log_softmax(s_logits / self.T, dim=-1)
        soft_teacher = F.softmax(t_logits / self.T, dim=-1)
        kd_loss = F.kl_div(soft_student, soft_teacher, reduction='batchmean') * (self.T ** 2)

        # Hard label loss
        ce_loss = F.cross_entropy(s_logits, flat_labels)

        # Combined
        loss = self.alpha * kd_loss + (1 - self.alpha) * ce_loss

        loss.backward()
        self.optimizer.step()
        self.optimizer.zero_grad()

        return loss.item(), kd_loss.item(), ce_loss.item()
```

### Response Distillation via SFT (Black-Box)

Use this when you only have API access to the teacher.

**Step 1: Generate training data from the teacher**

```python
import json
from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()  # or any API-compatible client

def generate_teacher_data(prompts, model="gpt-4", output_file="teacher_data.jsonl"):
    with open(output_file, "w") as f:
        for prompt in prompts:
            response = client.chat.completions.create(
                model=model,
                messages=[
                    {"role": "system", "content": "Think step by step. Explain your reasoning."},
                    {"role": "user", "content": prompt}
                ],
                temperature=0.7,
            )
            f.write(json.dumps({
                "prompt": prompt,
                "completion": response.choices[0].message.content,
            }) + "\n")
```

**Step 2: SFT the student on teacher data**

```python
from trl import SFTTrainer, SFTConfig
from datasets import load_dataset
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer
```

### Practical Tips

**Temperature selection**:
- T=1-3: For small teacher-student gaps (e.g., 13B → 7B). The teacher distribution is already informative.
- T=3-8: For medium gaps (e.g., 70B → 7B). Need more softening to extract useful signal.
- T=10-20: For very peaked distributions (e.g., highly fine-tuned teacher that's very confident). Rarely needed for LLMs.
- Start with T=4 and tune. Monitor whether the student is actually learning structure from the soft targets.

**Student architecture choices**:
- Same architecture family as teacher usually works best (Qwen teacher → Qwen student)
- The student should have enough capacity to absorb the knowledge — too small and it can't represent what the teacher knows
- Rule of thumb: 1/4 to 1/10 of teacher size for good results
- DeepSeek found diminishing returns above 32B for distilling from 671B — the marginal knowledge doesn't justify the extra parameters

**Data quantity vs quality**:
- More data isn't always better. Phi-1 used 7B tokens vs typical 1T+ pretraining
- Quality filtering matters enormously — reject incorrect outputs, deduplicate, remove noise
- For CoT distillation: rejection sampling (generate many, keep correct) dramatically improves data quality
- ~100k-800k high-quality samples is the typical range for effective distillation
- DeepSeek used ~800k samples; Orca used ~5M (but with lower quality per sample)

**Learning rate and training**:
- Use lower learning rates than standard SFT (1e-5 to 5e-5) to avoid catastrophic forgetting
- 1-3 epochs is typical; watch for overfitting on small distillation datasets
- Gradient accumulation helps when the distillation dataset is large and you're memory-constrained

## Distillation vs Other Compression Methods

Knowledge distillation is one of several approaches to making models smaller and faster. They're complementary.

| Method | What It Does | Quality Impact | Speed | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-------|-------------|
| **Distillation** | Train a smaller model to mimic a larger one | Moderate loss (retains 85-95% capability) | Train once, always fast | When you need a fundamentally smaller model |
| **Quantization** | Reduce numerical precision (FP16 → INT8 → INT4) | Small loss (retains 95-99% capability) | 2-4x faster, 2-4x less memory | Always, as a post-training step |
| **Pruning** | Remove redundant weights/neurons/attention heads | Moderate loss | 1.5-3x fewer parameters | When targeting specific hardware constraints |
| **Architecture distillation** | Design a new, efficient architecture informed by the teacher | Varies | Depends on architecture | Research settings, new model families |

**The recommended compression pipeline**:

```
Large Teacher (70B, FP16)
    │
    ▼ Distillation
Small Student (7B, FP16)    ← Biggest quality drop here, but biggest size reduction
    │
    ▼ Quantization
Small Student (7B, INT4)    ← Minimal additional quality loss, 4x memory reduction
    │
    ▼ (Optional) Pruning
Pruned Student (5B, INT4)   ← Moderate additional quality loss, further size reduction
```

Distill first, then quantize. Distillation is best at transferring *knowledge*; quantization is best at reducing *memory*. Trying to quantize a 70B model to INT4 (making it fit in memory) often loses more quality than distilling to a 7B FP16 model — because distillation can restructure knowledge for the smaller capacity, while quantization just crudely rounds numbers.

**GGUF/llama.cpp users**: The models you download as "Q4_K_M" or "Q8_0" are already quantized. If those models were also distilled (like R1-Distill-Qwen-7B-Q4), you're getting the benefits of both: a model distilled from 671B, then quantized to run on a laptop.
