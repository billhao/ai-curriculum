# Multimodal and Vision-Language Models (VLMs)

A guide to understanding how LLMs learn to see — from turning images into tokens, to training models that reason over both vision and language. Covers the full stack: vision encoders, projection layers, contrastive pretraining, visual instruction tuning, and the architectures behind GPT-4o, Gemini, LLaVA, and the open-source frontier.

## Background

**Foundational paper**: [An Image is Worth 16x16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929) (Dosovitskiy et al., Google Brain, 2020) — the Vision Transformer (ViT) that proved images could be processed as sequences of patches, just like tokens in a language model. This opened the door to unifying vision and language in a single transformer.

**Research lineage** — VLMs evolved through several stages:

1. **ViT** (Dosovitskiy et al., Google, 2020, [arxiv 2010.11929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929)) — Applied the standard transformer encoder to image patches. An image is split into fixed-size patches, linearly embedded, and processed as a sequence. Achieved state-of-the-art on ImageNet when pretrained on large datasets (JFT-300M). Proved that the transformer architecture — the same one in your GPT-2 — generalizes to vision without any convolutions.

2. **CLIP** (Radford et al., OpenAI, 2021, [arxiv 2103.00020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.00020)) — Trained a vision encoder and text encoder jointly on 400M image-text pairs from the internet using contrastive learning. Created a shared embedding space where images and text can be directly compared. CLIP's ViT-L/14 became the default vision backbone for most early VLMs.

3. **Flamingo** (Alayrac et al., DeepMind, 2022, [arxiv 2205.10747](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.10747)) — First high-performance VLM. Used a frozen vision encoder (CLIP) + frozen LLM (Chinchilla), connected via gated cross-attention layers and a Perceiver Resampler. Achieved strong few-shot performance on vision-language tasks without fine-tuning the base models. Introduced the cross-attention approach to multimodal fusion.

4. **LLaVA** (Liu et al., UW/Microsoft, 2023, [arxiv 2304.08485](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.08485)) — The canonical open-source VLM. Simpler than Flamingo: a linear projection maps CLIP vision features directly into the LLM's embedding space. No cross-attention — visual tokens are simply concatenated with text tokens. Two-stage training: pretrain projector on image-caption pairs, then fine-tune end-to-end with visual instruction data generated by GPT-4. NeurIPS 2023 Oral.

5. **GPT-4V/4o** (OpenAI, 2023-2024) — GPT-4V added vision to GPT-4 via a separate vision encoder. GPT-4o went further: a natively multimodal model trained end-to-end on text, images, audio, and video simultaneously, with early fusion rather than bolting on separate encoders. Set the standard for commercial multimodal systems.

6. **Gemini** (Google DeepMind, 2023-2026) — Natively multimodal from the start, trained on interleaved text, image, audio, and video data. Gemini 1.5 Pro introduced a 1M-token context window handling long video/document inputs. Gemini 2.5 Pro pushed reasoning capabilities, and Gemini 3 (2025) further advanced multimodal science and agent tasks.

7. **Open-source frontier** (2024-2026) — Qwen2.5-VL, InternVL3, LLaVA-NeXT, Pixtral, DeepSeek-VL2, and Llama 4 Scout brought open-weight VLMs to parity with commercial models on many benchmarks.

**The arc**: CNN-based vision → ViT (images as patches) → CLIP (shared vision-language space) → Flamingo (cross-attention fusion) → LLaVA (simple projection + concatenation) → GPT-4o/Gemini (native early-fusion multimodal). Each step simplified the architecture while expanding capabilities.

## Key Terms

**Vision encoder**: A model that converts raw pixels into a sequence of feature vectors. Almost always a ViT (Vision Transformer) pretrained with CLIP or SigLIP. Takes an image in, outputs a grid of feature vectors — one per image patch.

**Patch**: A square region of the input image. ViT splits an image into a grid of non-overlapping patches (typically 14x14 or 16x16 pixels each). Each patch becomes one "token" for the transformer.

**Visual tokens**: The patch embeddings that come out of the vision encoder. They live in the same sequence space as text tokens once projected into the LLM. A single image typically produces 196-1024 visual tokens depending on resolution and patch size.

**Projection layer (connector)**: A small network that maps vision encoder outputs into the LLM's embedding space. Can be as simple as a single linear layer (LLaVA v1) or an MLP (LLaVA v1.5). This bridges the dimensionality gap: CLIP ViT-L outputs 1024-dim vectors, but Vicuna-7B expects 4096-dim inputs.

**Contrastive learning**: A training objective that pulls matching image-text pairs together and pushes non-matching pairs apart in a shared embedding space. The foundation of CLIP and SigLIP. Not generative — it doesn't produce text, it learns aligned representations.

**Visual instruction tuning**: Fine-tuning a VLM on instruction-following data that includes images. Analogous to the SFT stage you did with Dolly 15k, but with image-text pairs where the model must follow instructions about visual content ("Describe this image," "What's the error in this code screenshot?").

**Early fusion**: Interleaving or combining vision and text tokens at the input level, before they enter the transformer layers. GPT-4o does this — all modalities share the same transformer from the start.

**Late fusion**: Processing vision and text through separate encoders, then combining their outputs at a later stage. Simpler but limits cross-modal interaction.

**Cross-attention fusion**: Inserting cross-attention layers into the LLM that attend to vision encoder outputs. The LLM's text tokens query against visual features. Used by Flamingo, Idefics, and others. More expressive than concatenation but adds architectural complexity.

**Concatenation fusion**: The simplest approach — project visual tokens into the LLM's embedding space and concatenate them with text tokens. The LLM's standard self-attention handles all cross-modal interaction. Used by LLaVA, Qwen-VL, InternVL, and most modern open-source VLMs.

## Vision Transformer (ViT) — Images as Tokens

The core insight: an image is just a sequence of patches, like a sentence is a sequence of tokens. If transformers work for language, they work for vision — with minimal modifications.

### Patch Embedding: A Numerical Walkthrough

Consider a standard 224x224 RGB image with 16x16 patches (the ViT-B/16 configuration):

```
Input image: 224 x 224 x 3 (height x width x RGB channels)
Patch size:  16 x 16

Number of patches = (224 / 16) x (224 / 16) = 14 x 14 = 196

Each patch: 16 x 16 x 3 = 768 values (flattened pixel values)
```

The image gets chopped into a 14x14 grid:

```
224 pixels
┌────┬────┬────┬────┬────┬─ ─ ─┬────┐
│ P1 │ P2 │ P3 │ P4 │ P5 │     │P14 │  ↑
├────┼────┼────┼────┼────┼─ ─ ─┼────┤  │
│P15 │P16 │P17 │    │    │     │P28 │  │
├────┼────┼────┼────┼────┼─ ─ ─┼────┤  224
│P29 │    │    │    │    │     │    │  pixels
├────┼────┼────┼────┼────┼─ ─ ─┼────┤  │
│    │    │    │    │    │     │    │  │
├─ ─ ┼─ ─ ┼─ ─ ┼─ ─ ┼─ ─ ┼─ ─ ─┼─ ─ ┤  │
│P183│    │    │    │    │     │P196│  ↓
└────┴────┴────┴────┴────┴─ ─ ─┴────┘
  16px each
```

Each patch is projected to a fixed embedding dimension via a linear layer:

```
Step 1: Flatten each patch
  Patch P1: 16 x 16 x 3 = 768-dim vector

Step 2: Linear projection (learned)
  E = W_proj @ patch + b_proj
  W_proj: 768 x D  (D = 768 for ViT-B, 1024 for ViT-L)

Step 3: Add [CLS] token (prepended)
  Sequence: [CLS, E1, E2, ..., E196]  →  197 tokens

Step 4: Add positional embeddings (learned, same dim as patch embeddings)
  Final input: 197 x D
```

This is nearly identical to how your GPT-2 processes text tokens — the main difference is that GPT-2 looks up token embeddings from a vocabulary table, while ViT linearly projects raw pixel patches. After embedding, both are just sequences of vectors processed by transformer layers.

### Parameter Count Comparison

```
Component             ViT-B/16     ViT-L/14     ViT-H/14
─────────────────    ──────────   ──────────   ──────────
Embedding dim (D)     768          1024         1280
Layers                12           24           32
Attention heads       12           16           16
Patch size            16x16        14x14        14x14
Patches (224x224)     196          256          256
Parameters            86M          304M         632M
```

ViT-L/14 (used by CLIP) produces 256 tokens of dimension 1024 for a 224x224 image. At higher resolutions (e.g., 336x336, which LLaVA uses), you get (336/14)^2 = 576 tokens.

### The [CLS] Token vs. All Patch Tokens

ViT was originally designed for classification — the [CLS] token's output is a single vector summarizing the whole image. For VLMs, we typically use **all patch tokens** (not just [CLS]) because the LLM needs spatial information — it needs to know *where* things are in the image, not just a global summary.

```
Classification (ViT original):
  [CLS] → single 1024-dim vector → MLP head → class label
  (only global information preserved)

VLM usage:
  [E1, E2, ..., E256] → 256 x 1024 → projection → 256 visual tokens for LLM
  (spatial information preserved — LLM knows patch 1 is top-left, etc.)
```

## CLIP — Contrastive Vision-Language Alignment

CLIP solved a key problem: how do you create a shared embedding space where images and text are directly comparable? Before CLIP, vision models and language models lived in entirely separate representation spaces.

### The Contrastive Objective

Given a batch of N image-text pairs, CLIP trains two encoders to maximize the similarity of correct pairs and minimize similarity of incorrect pairs:

```
Batch of N=4 image-text pairs:

Images:  I1  I2  I3  I4     Text:  T1  T2  T3  T4
  │                            │
  ▼                            ▼
Vision Encoder              Text Encoder
(ViT-L/14)                  (Transformer)
  │                            │
  ▼                            ▼
Image embeddings            Text embeddings
(N x D)                     (N x D)
  │                            │
  └──────────┬─────────────────┘
             ▼
       Cosine similarity matrix (N x N):

              T1      T2      T3      T4
        ┌────────┬────────┬────────┬────────┐
   I1   │  0.95  │  0.12  │  0.08  │  0.15  │  ← maximize diagonal
        ├────────┼────────┼────────┼────────┤
   I2   │  0.10  │  0.92  │  0.20  │  0.11  │
        ├────────┼────────┼────────┼────────┤
   I3   │  0.05  │  0.18  │  0.88  │  0.09  │
        ├────────┼────────┼────────┼────────┤
   I4   │  0.14  │  0.07  │  0.11  │  0.91  │  ← minimize off-diagonal
        └────────┴────────┴────────┴────────┘
```

The loss is symmetric cross-entropy — applied in both directions (image→text and text→image):

```
Loss = 0.5 * (CE_image_to_text + CE_text_to_image)

Where CE is cross-entropy treating the diagonal as the correct class.
Each image classifies which text matches (1-of-N), and vice versa.
```

CLIP used a batch size of 32,768 — meaning each image is contrasted against 32,767 negative text descriptions per step. This massive batch size is critical: more negatives = harder contrastive task = better representations.

### Training Scale

```
Dataset:     400M image-text pairs (WebImageText, scraped from internet)
Batch size:  32,768
Compute:     256 V100 GPUs, ~12 days for ViT-L/14
Temperature: Learnable scalar τ (initialized at 0.07), controls sharpness of softmax
```

### SigLIP — The Successor

SigLIP (Google, 2023) replaced CLIP's softmax-based contrastive loss with a **sigmoid loss** — each pair is independently classified as matching or not, rather than competing across the batch. This removes the need for large batch sizes and negative mining, simplifying training. SigLIP 2 (2025) added self-distillation, masked prediction, and multilingual training, and has become the preferred vision encoder for new VLMs. SigLIP 2 models range from 86M (ViT-B) to 1B (ViT-g) parameters.

### What CLIP Gives Us

CLIP's vision encoder is not generative — it does not produce text. It gives us:

1. **A pretrained vision encoder** that understands visual semantics, not just pixels
2. **A shared embedding space** where "a photo of a dog" and an actual dog photo land near each other
3. **Zero-shot transfer** — classify images into categories the model never saw during training by comparing image embeddings to text embeddings of category names

For VLMs, we take CLIP's vision encoder and throw away the text encoder. The LLM replaces CLIP's text encoder entirely, gaining generative capability while inheriting CLIP's visual understanding.

## LLaVA — The Canonical Open-Source VLM

LLaVA (Large Language-and-Vision Assistant) proved that you don't need complex cross-attention mechanisms — a simple linear projection from vision to language space, combined with visual instruction tuning, is enough to build a capable VLM.

### Architecture

```
                   Image (e.g., 336 x 336)
                           │
                           ▼
              ┌─────────────────────────┐
              │  CLIP ViT-L/14 @ 336px  │
              │  (frozen or fine-tuned)  │
              │                         │
              │  Output: 576 x 1024     │
              │  (576 patches, 1024-dim) │
              └────────────┬────────────┘
                           │
                           ▼
              ┌─────────────────────────┐
              │   Projection Layer      │
              │   MLP: 1024 → 4096      │
              │   (2-layer MLP in v1.5) │
              │                         │
              │   Output: 576 x 4096    │
              └────────────┬────────────┘
                           │
                           ▼
              ┌─────────────────────────┐
              │   Token Concatenation   │
              │                         │
              │  [SYS] [visual tokens]  │
              │  [USER] What is this?   │
              │  [ASSISTANT]            │
              │                         │
              │  = 576 visual + ~20     │
              │    text tokens          │
              └────────────┬────────────┘
                           │
                           ▼
              ┌─────────────────────────┐
              │   LLM (Vicuna-7B/13B)   │
              │   Standard transformer  │
              │   Self-attention over    │
              │   ALL tokens (visual +  │
              │   text) uniformly       │
              │                         │
              │   Output: next-token    │
              │   prediction            │
              └─────────────────────────┘
```

The key simplification: visual tokens and text tokens are treated identically by the LLM. The self-attention mechanism — the same one in your GPT-2 — handles all cross-modal interaction. No new architectural components needed in the LLM.

### Two-Stage Training

This is analogous to the pretrain → SFT pipeline you already know, but applied to the vision-language connection:

**Stage 1: Feature Alignment (Pretrain the Projector)**

```
Frozen:   CLIP ViT-L/14 (vision encoder)
Frozen:   Vicuna-7B (LLM)
Trained:  Projection layer only (a few million parameters)

Data:     558K image-caption pairs (filtered from CC3M)
Task:     Given image, generate the caption (standard next-token prediction)
Purpose:  Teach the projection layer to map CLIP features into
          a format the LLM can understand

Compute:  ~4 hours on 8x A100s
```

This stage is cheap because only the projection layer has gradients. The LLM and vision encoder are frozen — you're just learning the "translation" between two pretrained representation spaces.

**Stage 2: Visual Instruction Tuning (Fine-tune End-to-End)**

```
Frozen:   CLIP ViT-L/14 (vision encoder)
Trained:  Projection layer + LLM (Vicuna-7B, all parameters)

Data:     665K instruction-following samples:
          - 150K GPT-4-generated visual conversations
          - 515K academic VQA data

Task:     Follow instructions about images (describe, answer questions,
          reason about visual content)
Purpose:  Teach the model to follow visual instructions, not just caption

Compute:  ~10 hours on 8x A100s
```

This is SFT — the same thing you did with Dolly 15k and SlimOrca, but with images in the input. The loss is computed only on the assistant's response tokens, just like standard SFT with loss masking.

### LLaVA-1.5 Improvements

LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023) made targeted improvements:

```
Change                    LLaVA v1        LLaVA v1.5
─────────────────────    ───────────     ─────────────
Projection layer          Linear          2-layer MLP
Image resolution          224 x 224       336 x 336
Visual tokens             196             576
LLM backbone              Vicuna-7B       Vicuna-7B/13B
Academic VQA data          No             Yes (515K)
```

The 2-layer MLP projector was the biggest win — it gives the model more capacity to transform visual features before they enter the LLM.

### Training on Your H800

Running LLaVA-style training on 1xH800 80GB:

```
Model          VRAM for Training     Feasible on 1xH800?
─────────     ──────────────────    ────────────────────
LLaVA-7B       ~45GB (bf16)         Yes, with batch=4
LLaVA-13B      ~72GB (bf16)         Tight, batch=1-2
LLaVA-7B       ~28GB (LoRA r=64)    Yes, comfortably
```

With LoRA (which you can apply to the LLM while fully training the projector), even 13B models fit on a single H800. The vision encoder is typically frozen during fine-tuning, so it adds VRAM for forward pass only (no optimizer states).

## Cross-Attention vs. Concatenation

Two dominant approaches to fusing vision and language information in VLMs:

### Approach 1: Cross-Attention (Flamingo)

The LLM's text tokens *query* against visual features via dedicated cross-attention layers inserted between the LLM's existing layers:

```
Flamingo Architecture:

Vision Encoder ──► Perceiver Resampler ──► 64 visual tokens
                                                │
                                                │ (keys, values)
                                                ▼
LLM Layer 1:  Self-Attention ──► Cross-Attention ──► FFN
LLM Layer 2:  Self-Attention ──────────────────────► FFN  (no xattn)
LLM Layer 3:  Self-Attention ──► Cross-Attention ──► FFN
LLM Layer 4:  Self-Attention ──────────────────────► FFN  (no xattn)
              ...
              (cross-attention every other layer)
```

The **Perceiver Resampler** compresses the vision encoder's output (e.g., 256 tokens) down to a fixed set of 64 learned queries, regardless of image resolution. This controls the computational cost.

The **gated cross-attention** uses a learned scalar gate (initialized to zero, passed through tanh) that controls how much visual information flows into each layer. At initialization, the gate is zero — the LLM behaves exactly as if it were text-only. During training, the gate gradually opens, allowing visual information to influence text generation without destabilizing the pretrained LLM.

```
Gated cross-attention:

  gate = tanh(α)           # α is a learned scalar, initialized to 0
  h = h + gate * CrossAttn(h, visual_tokens)

  At init: gate ≈ 0 → no visual influence → stable LLM
  After training: gate ≈ 0.3-0.8 → controlled visual integration
```

**Advantages**: Efficient (fixed number of visual queries), preserves LLM weights exactly, visual tokens don't consume context length.

**Disadvantages**: More complex architecture, new parameters between every other layer, cross-attention layers must be trained from scratch.

### Approach 2: Concatenation (LLaVA)

Visual tokens are projected and concatenated directly with text tokens. The LLM's existing self-attention handles everything:

```
LLaVA Architecture:

Vision Encoder ──► Projection ──► 576 visual tokens
                                       │
                          ┌─────────────┘
                          ▼
Input sequence:  [visual_1, visual_2, ..., visual_576, user_text_tokens...]
                  ─────────────────────────────────── ─────────────────────
                  visual tokens                        text tokens

LLM Layer 1:  Self-Attention (over ALL tokens equally) ──► FFN
LLM Layer 2:  Self-Attention (over ALL tokens equally) ──► FFN
              ...
              (no architectural modifications to the LLM)
```

**Advantages**: Simple, no new architecture needed, full self-attention allows rich cross-modal interaction at every layer.

**Disadvantages**: Visual tokens consume context length (576 tokens per image is significant), computational cost scales quadratically with visual token count.

### Comparison

```
Aspect                 Cross-Attention            Concatenation
                       (Flamingo)                 (LLaVA)
────────────────────  ────────────────────────   ──────────────────────
Architecture change    New xattn layers           None (projection only)
Visual tokens in ctx   No (separate pathway)      Yes (consume context)
Context length cost    Constant (64 queries)      Linear (576+ per image)
Cross-modal depth      Every other layer          Every layer (full attn)
Training complexity    Must train xattn layers    Standard SFT pipeline
Open-source adoption   Less common                Dominant approach
Multi-image scaling    Efficient                  Expensive
```

**The industry converged on concatenation** for most use cases — its simplicity wins. LLaVA, Qwen-VL, InternVL, LLaMA Vision, and DeepSeek-VL all use concatenation. Cross-attention is preferred when context length is precious (multi-image, video) or when you cannot modify the LLM at all.

## Video Understanding — From Images to Temporal Sequences

Extending VLMs to video means handling a temporal dimension: the model must understand not just what's in a frame but how things change over time.

### The Token Explosion Problem

A single image produces ~576 visual tokens. A 30-second video at 1 FPS is 30 frames = 17,280 tokens. At 30 FPS = 518,400 tokens. This exceeds most LLMs' context windows and makes self-attention computationally intractable.

```
Token budget reality:

Source                 Tokens         Context cost
────────────────────  ──────────    ──────────────────
1 image (336x336)      576           ~1% of 128K context
30s video @ 1 FPS      17,280        ~13% of 128K context
30s video @ 30 FPS     518,400       Exceeds most contexts
5 min video @ 1 FPS    172,800       Needs 1M+ context
```

### Frame Sampling Strategies

Every video VLM must decide which frames to process:

**Uniform sampling**: Select N frames evenly spaced across the video. Simple, but misses fast actions and wastes tokens on static segments. Most common approach — LLaVA-Video and Qwen2.5-VL use this.

**Keyframe extraction**: Use scene-change detection or motion analysis to select informative frames. Reduces redundancy but misses gradual changes.

**Generative frame sampling**: Recent work (GenS, 2025) uses an MLLM to iteratively select which frames to examine based on the question being asked. This is adaptive — for "What color is the car?" you need one frame, for "How does the person's expression change?" you need many.

### Temporal Token Handling

```
Approach 1: Frame-level concatenation (most common)

  Frame 1:  [v1_1, v1_2, ..., v1_576]
  Frame 2:  [v2_1, v2_2, ..., v2_576]
  Frame 3:  [v3_1, v3_2, ..., v3_576]
  ...
  Concatenate: [v1_1, ..., v1_576, v2_1, ..., v2_576, v3_1, ..., v3_576, text...]

  Problem: token count grows linearly with frames


Approach 2: Temporal token compression

  Pool or merge tokens across frames that show similar content.
  576 tokens/frame x 30 frames → compress to ~2000 tokens total.
  Methods: spatial-temporal pooling, slot-based aggregation, token pruning.

  Qwen2.5-VL: groups 2x2 spatial patches across 2 temporal frames
              → 8x compression (576 → 72 tokens per frame-pair)


Approach 3: Memory-based approaches

  Process frames sequentially, maintaining a compressed memory state.
  Each new frame updates the memory rather than adding tokens.
  Scales to very long videos but may lose fine-grained details.
```

### Current Video VLMs

```
Model              Max Frames   Strategy                     Video Benchmarks
────────────────  ──────────   ───────────────────────────  ──────────────────
Gemini 2.5 Pro     Long video   Native long-context (1M)     Leading
GPT-4o             Screenshots  Frame extraction              Strong
Qwen2.5-VL-72B    ~128 frames  Dynamic resolution + merge   66.0 VideoMME
LLaVA-Video-72B   ~100 frames  Uniform + synthetic data     66.8 LongVideoBench
InternVL3-78B      ~64 frames  Frame concat + compression   Strong
```

### The Synthetic Data Pipeline

Most visual instruction data is generated by prompting a strong model (GPT-4/GPT-4V) with images and asking it to produce conversations:

```
Synthetic Data Generation (LLaVA approach):

1. Take image from COCO/CC3M
2. Extract bounding boxes and captions (existing annotations)
3. Feed to GPT-4 (text-only) with prompt:
   "Given this image description and objects, generate
    a multi-turn conversation about the image."
4. GPT-4 produces: question-answer pairs, detailed descriptions,
   complex reasoning chains

Result: 150K high-quality visual conversations
Cost:   ~$500 in GPT-4 API calls (2023 prices)
```

ShareGPT4V improved this by using GPT-4V (with vision) directly:

```
ShareGPT4V Pipeline:

1. Feed actual images to GPT-4V (not just descriptions)
2. GPT-4V generates detailed captions seeing the real image
3. Train a captioning model on 100K GPT-4V outputs
4. Use trained captioner to scale to 1.2M high-quality captions
5. Use these as pretraining data for VLMs

Result: Better alignment because captions come from actual visual understanding,
        not inferred from bounding box annotations
```

This is knowledge distillation applied to vision — the same concept you studied with DeepSeek-R1 distillation, but for visual understanding rather than reasoning chains.

### Architecture Trends (2025-2026)

1. **Native multimodal pretraining**: GPT-4o and Gemini showed that training on all modalities from scratch (early fusion) outperforms bolting vision onto a text LLM. Open-source is following: Llama 4 Scout is natively multimodal.

2. **MoE for VLMs**: DeepSeek-VL2 and Llama 4 Scout use MoE architectures — combining the efficiency you studied in your MoE guide with multimodal capabilities. A 109B total parameter model with 17B active per token.

3. **Dynamic resolution**: Fixed-resolution processing (resize all images to 224x224) is obsolete. Qwen2.5-VL processes images at native resolution by dynamically tiling. InternVL uses dynamic tiling up to 4K resolution. This matters for OCR, document understanding, and fine-grained visual tasks.

4. **SigLIP replacing CLIP**: SigLIP 2 (2025) has become the preferred vision encoder for new models, offering better localization, multilingual support, and flexible resolution via NaFlex.

5. **Longer visual context**: Gemini handles 1-hour videos natively. Qwen2.5-VL compresses video tokens 8x via spatial-temporal merging. The goal: process entire documents and long videos without truncation.

6. **Test-time compute for vision**: InternVL3 applies test-time compute scaling (the same concept from o1 and R1 that you studied) to multimodal reasoning — thinking longer about hard visual problems.

- 12B-27B models: use LoRA (r=64-128) for the LLM, fully train the projector

### Key Implementation Details

**Projector training**: Always fully train the projection layer — it's small (a few million parameters) and critical for alignment. Never quantize or freeze it.

**Loss masking**: Same as your SFT training — compute loss only on assistant response tokens. The visual tokens and user prompt tokens are not supervised.

**Image preprocessing**: Resize to the vision encoder's expected resolution. For dynamic resolution models (Qwen2.5-VL), pass the native resolution and let the model handle tiling internally.

**Multi-image input**: For models using concatenation, each image adds 576+ tokens. With 5 images, that's 2,880+ visual tokens before any text. Cross-attention models (Flamingo-style) handle this more efficiently.

## Key Papers

1. **An Image is Worth 16x16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale** — Dosovitskiy et al., Google (2020). The Vision Transformer (ViT). Images as patch sequences, processed by a standard transformer encoder. [arxiv:2010.11929](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929)

2. **Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision** — Radford et al., OpenAI (2021). CLIP. Contrastive pretraining on 400M image-text pairs, creating a shared vision-language embedding space. The vision encoder that launched the VLM era. [arxiv:2103.00020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.00020)

3. **Flamingo: a Visual Language Model for Few-Shot Learning** — Alayrac et al., DeepMind (2022). Gated cross-attention fusion between frozen vision and language models. Perceiver Resampler for fixed-size visual token compression. NeurIPS 2022. [arxiv:2205.10747](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.10747)

4. **Visual Instruction Tuning** — Liu et al., UW/Microsoft (2023). LLaVA. Simple linear projection + visual instruction tuning. Two-stage training pipeline. Defined the dominant open-source VLM architecture. NeurIPS 2023 Oral. [arxiv:2304.08485](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.08485)

5. **Improved Baselines with Visual Instruction Tuning** — Liu et al. (2023). LLaVA-1.5. MLP projector, higher resolution (336px), academic VQA data. Showed that simple, targeted improvements matter more than architectural complexity. CVPR 2024. [arxiv:2310.03744](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03744)

6. **Sigmoid Loss for Language Image Pre-Training** — Zhai et al., Google (2023). SigLIP. Replaced CLIP's softmax contrastive loss with sigmoid loss, removing batch-size dependency. Became the preferred vision encoder for modern VLMs. [arxiv:2303.15343](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.15343)

7. **SigLIP 2: Multilingual Vision-Language Encoders with Improved Semantic Understanding** — Google (2025). Self-distillation, masked prediction, multilingual, NaFlex for flexible resolution. State-of-the-art vision encoder. [arxiv:2502.14786](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14786)

8. **GPT-4 Technical Report** — OpenAI (2023). First GPT-4 with vision capability. Closed-source but set performance benchmarks. [arxiv:2303.08774](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.08774)

9. **GPT-4o System Card** — OpenAI (2024). Natively multimodal omni-model. Early fusion across text, image, audio, video. Defined the "native multimodal" paradigm. [openai.com/gpt-4o-system-card](https://cdn.openai.com/gpt-4o-system-card.pdf)

10. **Gemini: A Family of Highly Capable Multimodal Models** — Google DeepMind (2023). Natively multimodal, trained on interleaved data. 1M context window in 1.5 Pro. [arxiv:2312.11805](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.11805)

11. **Qwen2.5-VL Technical Report** — Qwen Team, Alibaba (2025). Dynamic resolution, native video understanding, spatial-temporal token merging, object grounding. Leading open-source VLM. [arxiv:2502.13923](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13923)

12. **InternVL3: Exploring Advanced Training and Test-Time Recipes for Open-Source Multimodal Models** — OpenGVLab (2025). InternViT-6B vision encoder, test-time compute scaling for multimodal reasoning. State-of-the-art open-source MMMU. [arxiv:2504.10479](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.10479)

13. **LLaVA-Video: Video Instruction Tuning With Synthetic Data** — LLaVA Team (2024). Extended LLaVA to video with 178K synthetic video instruction data. [arxiv:2410.02713](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02713)

14. **ShareGPT4V: Improving Large Multi-Modal Models with Better Captions** — Chen et al. (2023). GPT-4V-generated captions scaled via a trained captioner. Showed that higher-quality pretraining captions improve VLM performance. [arxiv:2311.12793](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12793)

15. **DeepSeek-VL2: Mixture-of-Experts Vision-Language Models for Advanced Multimodal Understanding** — DeepSeek (2024). First major MoE VLM, strong scientific reasoning. [arxiv:2412.10302](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.10302)
