# Architectures & Training for a Cognitive Core

Grounding: this note is based on reports 00-03 in `knowledge-vs-intelligence/`, the 12 existing open questions in `interp/open-research-questions.md`, and context from `interp/knowledge-vs-intelligence.md` and `arc-agi-2-guide.md`. I novelty-checked against the main adjacent 2025-2026 papers rather than treating "small core + retrieval" as novel by default: Han et al. (2502.19402), RARE (2503.23513), Search-R1 (2503.09516), SEM (2505.07903), Venhoff et al. (2510.07364), Yang et al. (2507.18178), Minitron (2407.14679), Memory Decoder (2508.09874), MemoryLLM (2602.00398), Training Dynamics of Parametric and In-Context Knowledge Utilization (2510.02370), Search-R3 (2510.07048), PAR^2-RAG (2603.29085), and Reasoning Memory / Procedural Knowledge at Scale (2604.01348).

## Proposal 1: Depth-Factored Recurrent Core with a Detachable Memory Trunk   [confidence/risk: medium-high confidence on the pilot; medium risk that factual knowledge leaks into the recurrent crown unless the bottleneck is enforced aggressively.]
- **Overview** — Build a model that bakes the reports' mechanistic claim directly into the architecture: lower layers are optimized to behave like a detachable memory trunk, while upper layers are replaced with a small looped/recurrent "reasoning crown" that provides effective depth at inference time. Retrieval and tool access connect only to the crown, not to the whole network, so factual access and serial computation are separated by construction rather than only by interpretation. The target artifact is a 1.5B-3B model whose reasoning quality depends mainly on recurrent depth, while factual behavior can be changed by swapping the trunk or the external store.
- **Motivation** — Report 01 argues that reasoning is best modeled as added serial computation / effective depth rather than a separate store, with direct support from looped transformers (2502.17416) and Venhoff's "when, not how" result (2510.07364). Report 02 highlights two engineering clues pointing in the same direction: knowledge-heavy capability degrades before lightweight reasoning under compression, and width pruning is less harmful than depth pruning in Minitron (2407.14679). Report 02 also notes that lower layers appear more knowledge-heavy and higher layers more reasoning-heavy, which aligns with the empirical layer split reported in 2507.18178. If that picture is right, the obvious next step is to train an architecture that makes this asymmetry load-bearing instead of merely observed after the fact.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — If the bottom 60-75% of a decoder-only LM is trained under memory-style objectives and then frozen or lightly tuned, while the top 25-40% is replaced by a shared recurrent block trained under reasoning-only distillation/RL objectives, then: 1) factual QA should depend disproportionately on the lower trunk, 2) algorithmic and multi-step reasoning should scale with the number of recurrent loops, and 3) swapping/compressing the trunk should hurt factual recall much more than procedural reasoning. A concrete implementation is: frozen lower transformer stack; top shared block looped 3-8 times with ACT-style halting; retrieval cross-attention only in the looped crown; layer-restricted losses so lower layers solve cloze/extraction and upper layers solve plan/verification/manipulation tasks.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Prior work gets partway there but not this far. The modular architecture paper with generalized cross-attention (2501.00823) is mostly a theoretical reframing of FFNs as implicit retrieval; it does not enforce detachability with training-time asymmetry or recurrent depth. RARE (2503.23513) externalizes knowledge and internalizes reasoning patterns, but still uses a standard monolithic student. Memory Decoder (2508.09874) and MemoryLLM (2602.00398) make memory more modular, but they are aimed at domain adaptation / FFN interpretability, not at a small recurrent reasoning core whose capability scales with loops and survives memory swapping. What is new here is the combination of: explicit depth factorization, retrieval restricted to the upper recurrent block, and detachability as the primary success criterion.
- **Proposed experiments** — Base models: Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-3B as the main pilots; a 7B ablation if compute allows. Teachers: released DeepSeek-R1/QwQ-class traces or public distilled reasoning corpora, plus a stronger open teacher for a held-out subset. Training protocol: freeze the lower trunk; replace the top `N` layers with a 2-layer shared block looped `L` times; train LoRA on the recurrent block, cross-attention, and halting head. Data: 1) factual extraction/manipulation pairs from synthetic biographies and Wiki-style cloze sets, 2) distilled reasoning traces from math/code/logic, 3) retrieval-conditioned multi-hop tasks. Baselines: vanilla LoRA on the full model, RARE-style retrieval-conditioned SFT (2503.23513), a non-recurrent top-stack control, and a Memory Decoder-style plug-in memory (2508.09874). Metrics: AIME24/MATH-500/LiveCodeBench or MBPP for procedural reasoning; SimpleQA/MMLU-Pro/GPQA-Diamond for factual load; HotpotQA/MuSiQue/BRIGHT/MQuAKE for retrieved composition; plus a new detachability metric: performance delta after swapping/compressing the lower trunk while keeping the crown fixed. Single-H800 pilot: Qwen2.5-1.5B, 4-bit QLoRA, looped top block over the last 6-8 layers, 50k-100k mixed examples, local BM25+dense retrieval, 2-4 day run.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect a clean asymmetry: reasoning metrics should improve with extra loops even when the trunk is fixed, while factual QA should be much more sensitive to trunk swaps or pruning. A strong positive result would be the first evidence that effective depth can be isolated into a compact crown without carrying most broad knowledge in the same parameters. The informative negative result is also valuable: if the crown rapidly re-memorizes facts or trunk swapping destroys reasoning, then the reports' apparent lower/upper layer asymmetry is descriptive but not architecturally actionable. The main risk is leakage: the recurrent crown may simply become a second memory store unless the retrieval restriction and layer losses are strong enough.

## Proposal 2: Counterfactual World-Swap Distillation for Fact-Invariant Reasoning   [confidence/risk: medium confidence; high upside if it works, but high risk that latent-plan alignment is too noisy to optimize cleanly.]
- **Overview** — Train the core on paired "same procedure, different world" examples so that the reasoning plan is encouraged to stay invariant while the factual grounding changes. Instead of teaching a model one chain of thought per problem, teach it that the same abstract program should survive a swap of entities, dates, laws, scientific constants, or temporal snapshots, with only the grounding steps changing. The core idea is to distill invariance over the plan, not just correctness of the final answer.
- **Motivation** — Report 01 and the counterfactual literature both point at the same weakness: models often look like reasoners until the facts are perturbed. MQuAKE (2305.14795) shows that even successful fact edits fail to propagate through multi-hop reasoning. "Reasoning or Reciting?" (2307.02477) and the 2025 counterfactual study on parametric knowledge (2506.15732) both show substantial degradation when default factual scaffolding is replaced with counterfactual variants. Report 03 adds an important training insight: distillation can add genuinely new patterns where plain RLVR mostly sharpens existing ones (2504.13837). That suggests a concrete intervention: distill not just answers or verbal traces, but cross-world invariants in the reasoning trajectory itself.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — If a student is trained on paired problems `(x_a, x_b)` that share an abstract solution graph but differ in factual world state, and is regularized so that its pre-grounding latent plan states align across the pair while its grounding states diverge appropriately, then it will generalize better under edits, temporal shifts, and retrieval conflicts. A concrete loss is `L = L_answer + L_trace + lambda * L_plan_align - mu * L_grounding_collapse`, where `L_plan_align` matches hidden states at teacher-identified plan checkpoints across worlds and `L_grounding_collapse` penalizes the student if world-specific checkpoints become too similar. This can be done with explicit CoT checkpoints or with a small set of learned "plan slots" decoded from hidden states.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Existing work mainly diagnoses the counterfactual failure but does not train against it in this structured way. 2506.15732 shows that simple post-hoc finetuning struggles to instill counterfactual reasoning and can degrade stored knowledge. MQuAKE (2305.14795) and ReCoE (2401.17585) are evaluation lenses, not training methods. Recent reasoning-distillation papers focus on exposure bias and student-teacher mismatch, e.g. MOTAB (2605.19433), but not on explicit world-swap invariance. The frontier move here is to treat factual substitutions as interventional supervision over the reasoning process itself, closer to causal representation learning than to ordinary CoT distillation.
- **Proposed experiments** — Data construction: 1) synthetic relation graphs and biography worlds where the same reasoning template is instantiated with different entity assignments, 2) temporal Wikipedia/Wikidata snapshots where the same question has different answers across years, 3) domain-local world swaps for law/biomed where the inferential pattern is stable but the facts differ. Models: Qwen2.5-1.5B or 3B student; teachers can be released reasoning checkpoints or offline-generated traces from a stronger open model. Baselines: ordinary SFT on the same paired data without alignment loss, DPO/GRPO-only post-training, and retrieval-only augmentation. Metrics: MQuAKE, ReCoE, temporal QA, counterfactual variants of HotpotQA/MuSiQue, plus standard reasoning benchmarks to make sure we are not just learning a niche skill. Key ablations: align only token traces vs align hidden checkpoints; explicit CoT vs latent plan slots; pairwise world swaps vs single-world supervision. Single-H800 pilot: 1.5B QLoRA on 100k-200k synthetic/temporal pairs, with checkpoint alignment computed only on 4-8 plan positions per sample.
- **Expected outcome** — My prediction is that this will not dramatically raise raw MMLU or SimpleQA, but it should materially improve edit propagation, temporal robustness, and retrieved multi-hop consistency relative to equally sized distilled baselines. A positive result would challenge the strong form of "post-training only teaches when" by showing that interventional distillation can add a more abstract, fact-invariant reasoning operator than ordinary trace imitation does. The informative negative result is that if even paired counterfactual supervision fails, then the entanglement is deeper than a training signal problem and likely lives in the substrate or base architecture. The main risk is that latent-plan alignment becomes brittle or overconstrains the student into a teacher-specific trace geometry.

## Proposal 3: Swappable Knowledge Shards with Reasoning-Invariance Training   [confidence/risk: medium confidence on feasibility; medium-high risk that shard routing becomes another brittle expert-selection problem.]
- **Overview** — Replace the vague idea of "external memory" with explicit source-addressable knowledge shards that can be added, removed, or updated without retraining the reasoning core. The dense core is trained primarily on reasoning/manipulation; factual content is moved into shard modules, ideally cheap LoRA/adapter experts in the single-H800 pilot and sparse experts in a larger follow-up. During training, the model is forced to preserve its reasoning trajectory under shard dropout and shard swapping, so the core does not silently re-absorb the facts.
- **Motivation** — Report 02's unlearning section is the most practical negative result in the packet: once knowledge and reasoning cohabit the same weights, deleting the former damages the latter. Report 02 also points out that small models can often use context better than they can store broad knowledge, and that no published system has cleanly demonstrated Karpathy's cognitive core. On the systems side, Memory Decoder (2508.09874), MemOS (2505.22101), and MemoryLLM (2602.00398) show growing interest in modular memory, but they are not designed around reasoning-preserving unlearning or detachable domain/time slices. The reports imply that if knowledge is to be cleanly removable, the separation likely has to be installed during training, not recovered afterward.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — A model with a dense reasoning core plus multiple source-specific knowledge shards, trained with shard dropout, shard swapping, and provenance supervision, will support much cleaner update/unlearning behavior than a monolithic model of the same total parameter budget. Concretely: the core answers via three channels, `param_core`, `shard_i`, and `retrieval`; a router predicts which shards are eligible; a provenance head predicts which channel should dominate. During training, random shard masking and cross-shard counterfactual swaps are applied, and a hidden-state consistency loss penalizes large changes in the core's pre-grounding reasoning when a shard changes. In the larger version, each shard is a sparse expert block; in the H800 pilot, each shard is a LoRA/adapter bank.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — This is adjacent to but distinct from current modular memory work. Memory Decoder (2508.09874) learns a plug-in memory that imitates retrieval for domain adaptation; it does not optimize for reasoning invariance or removability. MemoryLLM (2602.00398) makes FFNs interpretable as memory, but not explicitly swappable by source or time. The generalized cross-attention modular transformer (2501.00823) provides a formal lens, not an update/unlearning training recipe. The new piece here is operational: source/time/domain-partitioned knowledge shards, trained under explicit shard perturbations, with success defined by reasoning-preserving removal and update rather than just domain perplexity or QA accuracy.
- **Proposed experiments** — Pilot architecture: Qwen2.5-1.5B dense core plus 4-8 LoRA shards, partitioned by domain or by time slice. Example partitions: law, finance, biomed, current events; or 2019/2021/2023/2025 Wikipedia slices. Training: 1) shard-only factual cloze/QA on the domain slice, 2) core-only reasoning traces on math/code/logic and knowledge-manipulation tasks, 3) joint routing/provenance tuning on mixed factual and multi-hop questions, with random shard dropout and time-slice swaps. Baselines: full-model LoRA, Memory Decoder-style plugin memory (2508.09874), retrieval-only RAG, and a standard MoE with no invariance losses. Metrics: domain QA, temporal QA, MMLU subject subsets, MQuAKE/ReCoE after edits, plus reasoning retention on AIME/MATH/ARC-style symbolic tasks. Single-H800 pilot: sequentially train shard adapters, then train router/provenance/core heads with QLoRA; no full MoE pretraining required.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect this to be the most directly useful idea for updateability and reasoning-preserving unlearning. A strong result would look like this: removing the biomed shard tanks biomed factual QA but leaves math/code/ARC-style reasoning nearly unchanged, while swapping the 2023 shard for a 2025 shard updates temporal QA without broad regressions. That would be concrete evidence that decoupling is easier when installed as modular source-specific capacity than when recovered by post-hoc editing. The informative negative result is also important: if the core still caches enough shard knowledge that removal hardly matters, then the architecture has not really decoupled anything. The main risk is router brittleness and co-adaptation between shards and core.

## Proposal 4: Adversarial Arbitration Curriculum for Parametric-vs-Retrieved Knowledge   [confidence/risk: medium confidence; lower novelty than Proposals 1-3 because search-RL is now crowded, but still high value if framed around conflict arbitration rather than raw search skill.]
- **Overview** — Train the cognitive core not just to search, but to arbitrate between three actions: trust internal memory, retrieve, or abstain/defer. The curriculum intentionally injects conflicts between parametric knowledge, retrieved evidence, and distractors, and rewards source choice as well as answer correctness. The goal is to teach a compact reasoner the boundary condition of its own knowledge, not merely how to issue better search queries.
- **Motivation** — Report 02 is explicit that retrieval is often the bottleneck (BRIGHT, 2407.12883) and that multi-hop reasoning over retrieved facts often collapses (MQuAKE, 2305.14795). At the same time, Search-R1 (2503.09516) and SEM (2505.07903) show that post-training can improve search behavior, especially the decision of when not to search. The 2025 controlled study on parametric vs in-context arbitration (2510.02370) is especially important: it shows that inconsistent information and distributional skew can induce more robust arbitration strategies during training. The gap is that current search-RL work optimizes tool use, while the reports suggest the deeper problem is conflict arbitration under uncertainty.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — If we train a small reasoner on a balanced curriculum containing: 1) answerable-from-weights questions, 2) answerable-only-with-retrieval questions, and 3) conflict questions where weights and retrieved context disagree, with explicit rewards for correct source choice and for abstaining when neither is reliable, then the model will become substantially more robust under knowledge conflict than standard search-RL or RAG training. A concrete design is a three-head policy over `answer`, `search`, and `abstain`, with an auxiliary provenance classifier and an evidence-sufficiency reward. Retrieval itself can remain simple in the pilot; the main point is to teach the reasoner to recognize when search is necessary and when retrieved evidence should override stale parameters.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Search-R1 (2503.09516), SEM (2505.07903), Search-R3 (2510.07048), GlobalRAG (2510.20548), and PAR^2-RAG (2603.29085) all improve some aspect of search-aware reasoning, but none of them make parametric-vs-context conflict the central training object. 2510.02370 studies arbitration dynamics in controlled pretraining, but does not turn that insight into an explicit post-training curriculum for a knowledge-light reasoning core. This proposal is therefore narrower than "better RAG" and more specific than "teach search": it is a source-arbitration training recipe motivated by the reports' core claim that externalized knowledge only helps if the model knows when its own memory is insufficient or stale.
- **Proposed experiments** — Model: Qwen2.5-1.5B or 3B with a local BM25 plus a small dense retriever. Training data: synthetic biographies/relations with controlled contradictions; temporal QA where retrieved documents are newer than the model's likely parametric state; MuSiQue/HotpotQA-style multi-hop with adversarial distractors; and a clean subset where no search is needed. Rewards: answer correctness, minimal unnecessary search, correct provenance prediction, and evidence sufficiency. Baselines: standard RAG, Search-R1-style GRPO, SEM-style search-efficiency training, and an oracle pipeline with perfect retrieval. Metrics: accuracy, average searches/question, calibration/ECE, robustness to injected misinformation, and source-choice accuracy. Single-H800 pilot: 1.5B GRPO or DPO on a small local corpus with 50k-100k mixed examples and cached retrieval.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect this to produce modest gains on ordinary clean QA but large gains on conflict-heavy settings: temporal QA, noisy retrieval, and distractor-rich multi-hop. A positive result would turn the reports' "when, not how" claim into a more operational statement: for knowledge-light cores, a large part of intelligence may be learning when to trust memory, when to pull evidence, and when to defer. The informative negative result is that if conflict-arbitration training adds little over Search-R1/SEM, then the real wall is retrieval quality itself rather than arbitration. The main risk is benchmark leakage into reward design: the model may learn superficial source-selection heuristics rather than genuine epistemic arbitration.
