# Measurement & Mechanism Proposals — Knowledge vs Reasoning in LLMs

Grounding: I read the five required local reports in full, plus the two context skims, and novelty-checked against recent 2025-2026 work. These proposals are deliberately non-duplicative with the existing 12 open questions: I am not re-proposing RL-from-random-init, wholesale category ablation, end-to-end aha attribution, generic latent-CoT suppression, or generic cross-family universality.

## Proposal 1: Conditional Used-Capacity Tomography for Real Models   [confidence: high on scientific value; risk: medium because the causal estimator matters]
- **Overview** — The reports are explicit that nobody has measured the knowledge-vs-reasoning fraction of a real LLM; we only have a synthetic capacity ceiling and architectural heuristics. The key move is to stop asking for a static parameter split and instead measure a conditional, task-dependent split: for a given prompt, how much parameter mass and FLOPs are causally required for direct recall, for knowledge manipulation, and for knowledge-free reasoning with the same abstract structure? This turns the vague “what percent is knowledge?” question into an audit of used capacity rather than total capacity.
- **Motivation** — The reports emphasize three facts that make the current literature insufficient: (1) Allen-Zhu & Li’s ~2 bits/parameter law is a synthetic-capacity ceiling, not an allocation measurement (arXiv:2404.05405); (2) MLPs are a major factual store, but localization is not clean, because editability and use diverge (Hase et al., arXiv:2301.04213), relation knowledge also sits in attention (Wei et al., arXiv:2409.00617), and the reasoning/memorization tradeoff can ride a single residual direction (Hong et al., arXiv:2503.23084); (3) MoE capacity drops only modestly while using a small active parameter set, so “used capacity” and “installed capacity” are already visibly different (again arXiv:2404.05405). This matters because the field currently has no real-model accounting framework that distinguishes stored capacity, accessible capacity, and actually-used capacity.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — In real open-weight models, direct factual recall will use broader shallow storage mass, especially MLP-heavy mass, while reasoning/manipulation will use less unique parameter mass but more serial compute and deeper repeated pathways. The overlap will be substantial rather than cleanly separated, but asymmetric enough to estimate a meaningful conditional fraction. In MoEs, expert routing will make the same asymmetry easier to see at the active-subnetwork level than in dense models.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Closest prior work gives pieces, not the measurement we need: Allen-Zhu & Li measure capacity ceilings on synthetic data (arXiv:2404.05405); Morris et al. estimate memorization capacity by separating memorization from generalization (arXiv:2505.24832); Hong et al. study parameter specialization for knowledge storage (arXiv:2505.17260). None of these papers estimate a knowledge-vs-reasoning fraction on real open models by causal sufficiency, active parameters, and active FLOPs on matched task families. The novelty here is the combination of matched task design plus minimal-sufficient-subnetwork estimation plus dense-vs-MoE comparison.
- **Proposed experiments** — Build a controlled benchmark of matched task triplets over the same underlying fact graph: (1) direct recall, (2) manipulation over those facts (comparison, inverse lookup, 2-hop composition), and (3) a knowledge-free isomorphic version with placeholder symbols. Add a real-data slice using CounterFact/MQuAKE-style facts, SOCRATES-style shortcut-free multi-hop items (arXiv:2411.16679), and GSM-Symbolic-style perturbations for anti-template controls (arXiv:2410.05229). Run this on `Qwen2.5-7B`, `Gemma-2-9B`, `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B`, and one public MoE that fits comfortably on 80GB, e.g. `Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B`. For each correct answer, estimate a minimal sufficient mask over layer groups, MLP channels, attention heads, and experts that preserves the answer logit margin within epsilon, using iterative causal masking rather than pure saliency. Report: active parameter fraction, active FLOP fraction, overlap between recall/manipulation/isomorphic reasoning masks, depth distribution of the sufficient subnetwork, and route entropy for the MoE. A single-H800 pilot is feasible as an inference-only study on 5k synthetic items plus 1k real items; if the full sparse-mask search is too slow, restrict the search to layer groups and grouped channels first, then drill down only where the causal effect is large.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect a phase diagram, not a clean partition: recall should rely more on shallow storage-heavy mass; manipulation should reuse more of that mass but shift toward deeper compute; knowledge-free isomorphic reasoning should use the least unique storage mass and the most repeated mid/late-layer computation. If the masks almost fully overlap across all task types, that would be a strong negative result: even a conditional fraction is ill-posed, which would directly answer the reports’ central measurement gap. If the MoE shows a much cleaner conditional split than dense models, that would suggest the decomposition is architecturally realizable even if dense transformers obscure it.

## Proposal 2: Edit-Transport Commutators for Knowledge–Reasoning Separability   [confidence: medium-high on novelty; risk: medium because editing noise can confound interpretation]
- **Overview** — Current editing papers tell us that direct recall edits can work while downstream reasoning over the edited fact often fails. The deeper question is not just “does propagation fail?” but “where does separability break?” I would formalize separability as approximate commutativity between a knowledge intervention and a reasoning computation, then localize the failure layer-by-layer and mechanism-by-mechanism.
- **Motivation** — The reports already connect the relevant clues: localization is not the same as editability (Hase et al., arXiv:2301.04213); relation knowledge is stored differently from entity knowledge and significantly involves attention (Wei et al., arXiv:2409.00617); MQuAKE shows that edited facts do not propagate through multi-hop reasoning (arXiv:2305.14795); reasoning-model unlearning damages reasoning when it removes knowledge (R²MU, arXiv:2506.12963). What is still missing is a mechanistic criterion for “separable enough to compose.” Without that, the field can say that editing is brittle, but not whether the brittleness is in storage, in transport, or in composition.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — Define an edit operator `E` and a reasoning operator `R`. If knowledge storage and reasoning computation are modular enough, then `E` followed by `R` should approximately match the answer distribution obtained when reasoning is recomputed over the edited world state. The concrete hypothesis is that non-commutativity is concentrated not at the nominal fact-storage site but in attention-mediated transport and composition of relational state. Entity edits should commute farther than relation edits; activation-space and context-space interventions should fail later than weight-space edits if storage is more separable than transport.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Closest prior papers target performance, not the separability metric itself. MQuAKE diagnoses the symptom (arXiv:2305.14795). TRACK extends that symptom to conflicting knowledge under multi-step reasoning (arXiv:2601.15495). ACE improves propagation by editing query-value pathways (arXiv:2510.07896). None of them define a general commutator-style measure of knowledge/reasoning non-commutativity and use it to map where entanglement lives across edit types, relation types, and hop counts. That metric would be useful even if you never invent a better editor.
- **Proposed experiments** — Start with a fully controlled synthetic setup: train a 160M or 410M GPT-2-style transformer from scratch on synthetic biographies and relation graphs, so the data-generating process is known exactly. Build query families for direct recall, inverse relation, comparison, and `k`-hop composition. Apply four intervention types: weight editing (ROME/MEMIT-style), activation editing at the subject/relation token, retrieval/context substitution, and low-rank adapter editing. For each, compute direct edit success, `k`-hop transport success, and a commutator score across hops and relation families. During post-edit inference, selectively ablate attention vs MLP layers to see where the edited signal dies. Then transfer the same protocol to a public 1.5B-7B model on a CounterFact/MQuAKE/TRACK subset to see whether the synthetic commutator map predicts real-model failure modes. This fits a single H800 comfortably: the synthetic model can be trained and instrumented locally, and the 1.5B-7B transfer is inference-only.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect the main commutator spike to appear in relation transport and multi-hop composition rather than direct storage, especially in attention-heavy pathways. If so, the field should stop reading MQuAKE as “reasoning and knowledge are inseparable” and start reading it as “the composition channel is the bottleneck.” If the commutator is already large for hop-1 activation and context edits, that would be a much stronger negative result: entanglement is earlier and more structural than current editing papers imply. The most interesting positive surprise would be discovering relation classes that commute cleanly; that would give the first concrete boundary where separability is empirically real.

## Proposal 3: Counterfactual Trajectory Transplants to Separate WHEN from HOW   [confidence: high on importance; risk: medium-high because alignment between trajectories is technically delicate]
- **Overview** — The strongest current story is Venhoff’s: pretraining installs the reasoning machinery, and post-training mostly learns when to deploy it. That claim is causal but still coarse. The sharper test is to transplant selected hidden-state segments from a reasoning model into its base-model counterpart and ask which segment carries the performance gap: early trigger states, mid-chain algorithmic states, or late verification states.
- **Motivation** — The reports converge on Venhoff et al. recovering up to 91% of the thinking-model gap by steering only 12% of tokens (arXiv:2510.07364), on Yue et al. showing RL sharpens sampling more than expanding the reasoning frontier (arXiv:2504.13837), and on Ward et al. showing reasoning finetuning repurposes latent base representations rather than inventing them (arXiv:2507.12638). But the same reports also cite evidence that mid/late internal structure matters: correctness is encoded before verbalization (arXiv:2504.05419), and self-verification depends on identifiable heads/GLU components (arXiv:2504.14379). A further complication is that RL can unlock latent factual recall too, without chain-of-thought (arXiv:2605.07153), which raises the possibility that the “when not how” effect is not reasoning-specific at all.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — On easy-to-medium problems, transplanting only early “reasoning onset” states from the thinking model into the base model should recover most of the advantage. On harder tasks that genuinely require deeper internal serial computation or verification, early transplants will saturate early and mid/late transplants will add distinct gains. A second hypothesis is that if the same early trigger transplants also improve closed-book factual QA, then the phenomenon is broader than reasoning: post-training may be teaching a general “unlock useful low-probability trajectories” policy rather than a purely reasoning-specific scheduler.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Closest prior work steers or probes, but it does not decompose the full post-training delta by trajectory segment. Venhoff et al. intervene with steering vectors (arXiv:2510.07364). Ward et al. identify repurposed backtracking directions (arXiv:2507.12638). Zhang et al. and Lee et al. probe correctness and verification (arXiv:2504.05419, arXiv:2504.14379). None of these papers do direct cross-model transplants of early vs middle vs late hidden states between matched base/reasoning pairs and quantify gap recovery by segment. That makes this a sharper falsification of the central hypothesis than latent-CoT suppression alone.
- **Proposed experiments** — Use architecture-matched pairs such as `Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct` vs `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B` and `Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct` or `Qwen2.5-Math-7B` vs `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B`. Evaluate on five task buckets: direct factual QA, GSM8K/MATH-500 easy-medium, long state tracking/planning, SOCRATES-style shortcut-free multi-hop (arXiv:2411.16679), and a deliberately hard set such as MATH-Beyond (arXiv:2510.11653, if you include it, note that this is a harder stress test rather than a canonical benchmark). Use probes and token-level alignment to identify early onset, first verification, first backtrack, and final commitment points. Patch only early states, only mid states, only late states, and combinations; add sham patches from stylistically similar but incorrect reasoning traces. Report the percent of the thinking-model gap recovered, answer entropy, trace length, hidden-state self-verification score, and transfer of each transplant type to factual QA. This is feasible on one H800 as an inference-only study on 1k-2k prompts per bucket.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect a three-regime pattern. Early-only transplants should explain much of the gap on easier math and some factual-recall failures, which would support the reports’ “when not how” thesis. But on the hardest multi-hop and long-state tasks, I expect clear incremental gains from mid or late transplants, which would falsify the strongest version of that thesis and replace it with a weaker one: post-training is mostly about “when,” except when it also stabilizes verification and long-horizon state maintenance. If early-only transplants recover almost everything, that would be a cleaner and stronger confirmation of Venhoff than the current steering evidence.

## Proposal 4: MoE Standing-Committee vs Peripheral-Expert Audit   [confidence: medium; risk: medium on execution, high on interpretation if the routing signal is weak]
- **Overview** — Sparse MoEs are the one architecture where “used capacity” is explicit. That makes them the best real-model testbed for a knowledge-vs-reasoning audit. The core question is whether the active expert set for knowing facts differs causally from the active expert set for manipulating those facts, or whether the apparent specialization is mostly an illusion created by routing geometry.
- **Motivation** — The reports point out that Allen-Zhu’s MoE result is one of the few clues that knowledge can be stored sparsely without using most parameters per token (arXiv:2404.05405). New 2026 work now creates an opening: the “standing committee” paper argues that MoEs rely on a persistent domain-invariant expert core rather than strong domain specialization (arXiv:2601.03425), while EMO claims that a modular pretraining objective yields genuinely semantic expert groups with selective expert use (arXiv:2605.06663). If there is any place to do a real expert-level knowledge/reasoning audit, it is here.
- **Hypothesis / Idea** — Standard MoEs will show a stable committee that supports syntax, routing stability, and general reasoning control, plus peripheral experts that contribute domain-specific factual knowledge. However, multi-hop factual reasoning will require committee-periphery interaction, so even MoEs will not yield a clean factorization by default. EMO-style modular MoEs should show stronger expert-level separability than standard MoEs if separability is architecturally realizable rather than merely latent.
- **Why it is novel and at the frontier** — Closest prior work studies routing structure and modularity, not capability partition. The standing-committee paper asks whether specialization is an illusion (arXiv:2601.03425). EMO asks whether modularity can be induced during pretraining (arXiv:2605.06663). Neither paper asks whether the expert sets for direct recall, relation manipulation, multi-hop composition, and knowledge-free reasoning over the same structure are causally different, nor whether expert swapping selectively destroys one axis while preserving the other. That is the missing audit.
- **Proposed experiments** — Run a routing-and-ablation study on `Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B` and, if public and convenient, an EMO checkpoint such as the 1B-active/14B-total model from arXiv:2605.06663; include a dense 7B control with matched active FLOPs. Build matched task quadruples within the same domain: direct recall, relation manipulation, multi-hop composition, and a knowledge-free isomorphic reasoning version. Log top-`k` routes per token; define committee experts by route persistence across domains and tasks. Then do causal tests: ablate committee experts, ablate peripheral experts, swap peripheral experts across domains, freeze routing while perturbing expert outputs, and compare to matched random masks. Report route overlap, mutual information between experts and relation/domain/operator labels, selective impairment curves, and active-FLOP-normalized accuracy. A single-H800 pilot is feasible as an inference-only audit on `Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B` plus one dense control across 5k-10k items; the EMO extension is optional.
- **Expected outcome** — I expect standard MoEs to show only partial factorization: committee ablation hurts reasoning broadly, peripheral ablation hurts long-tail recall, and multi-hop factual reasoning needs both. If EMO yields a much cleaner split, that would be strong evidence that the entanglement in dense models is at least partly architectural rather than inevitable. If even EMO fails to separate these axes under causal tests, that would be strong negative evidence against the idea that sparse routing alone can produce a clean knowledge/reasoning decomposition.
